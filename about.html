Our Story

Over four decades of bold ideas shaping American public policy.

The Manhattan Institute for Policy Research is a leading free-market think tank whose mission is to develop and disseminate new ideas that foster greater economic choice and individual responsibility. Founded in 1977 by Antony Fisher and William J. Casey, the Institute has been at the forefront of America's most important policy debates for over four decades.

Our scholars and fellows produce rigorous, evidence-based research on the most pressing issues facing America's cities, states, and the nation. From urban policy and economic regulation to education reform and public safety, the Manhattan Institute works to turn bold ideas into meaningful policy change.

Our Mission

The Manhattan Institute's mission is to advance opportunity, individual liberty, and the rule of law in America and its great cities. We pursue this mission through research, publishing, and civic engagement, bringing together scholars, policymakers, journalists, and civic leaders to build a more prosperous, free, and just society.

We believe that free markets, personal responsibility, and the institutions of civil society are essential to human flourishing. Our work spans eight core policy areas: Cities, Culture, Economics, Education, Governance, Health, Public Safety, and Technology.

Reihan Salam has served as president of the Manhattan Institute since 2019. A contributing editor at National Review and the author of Melting Pot or Civil War?, Salam brings deep expertise in immigration policy, economic mobility, and the challenges facing America's middle class. Under his leadership, the Institute has expanded its research portfolio and strengthened its position as a leading voice in American policy debates.

Previously, Salam served as executive editor of National Review and as a policy analyst at the New America Foundation. He is a graduate of Harvard University.

Our History

From its founding to the present day, the Manhattan Institute has been a catalyst for ideas that improve American life.

1977

The Manhattan Institute is founded by Antony Fisher and William J. Casey (later CIA director) as the International Center for Economic Policy Studies. The Institute's early work focuses on promoting free-market economic principles.

1981

George Gilder's Wealth and Poverty, published with the Institute's support, becomes a national bestseller and an intellectual foundation for supply-side economics.

1984

The organization is renamed the Manhattan Institute for Policy Research, reflecting its broadened focus on domestic policy issues, particularly those affecting America's cities.

1990

The Institute launches City Journal, a quarterly magazine that becomes one of the most influential publications in urban policy. Under editor Myron Magnet, it helps shape the intellectual groundwork for New York City's dramatic turnaround in the 1990s.

1994

Charles Murray's The Bell Curve and other Institute-supported works spark national debates on welfare reform, education policy, and the role of government. MI scholars help build the intellectual case for welfare reform, culminating in the landmark 1996 legislation.

2000s

The Institute expands its focus to include education reform, legal reform, health-care policy, and technology policy. MI scholars play key roles in advancing school choice, tort reform, and market-based healthcare solutions.

2010s

MI launches major initiatives including Proxy Monitor, Trial Lawyers Inc., and the Adam Smith Society, broadening its influence in corporate governance, legal reform, and engaging the next generation of free-market leaders.

2019

Reihan Salam becomes the Institute's president, bringing a renewed focus on economic mobility, immigration policy, and the challenges facing middle-class Americans. The Institute expands its digital presence and public engagement.

2020s

The Manhattan Institute continues to shape national debates on urban policy, education reform, public safety, and the role of technology in society. New initiatives include the Gotham Renaissance project and the Logos Initiative.

Our Values

The Manhattan Institute is guided by a set of core principles that have remained constant since our founding:

Intellectual Rigor: We hold ourselves to the highest standards of evidence-based research. Our scholars produce work that is methodologically sound, clearly reasoned, and grounded in real-world data.

Independence: We are a nonpartisan organization committed to following the evidence wherever it leads. Our scholars represent diverse perspectives united by a commitment to free markets and individual liberty.

Impact: We measure our success not by the volume of our output but by the real-world impact of our ideas. From welfare reform to broken-windows policing to school choice, MI ideas have shaped American life.

Boldness: We are not afraid to challenge conventional wisdom, question sacred cows, or propose solutions that are creative and unconventional. Our tagline says it all: Creative. Bold. Independent.
